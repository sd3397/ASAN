About ASAN

ASAN is a non profit organization that aims to help with the self advocacy for adult autistic individuals. Unlike other Autism organizations, ASAN focuses on rights for autistic people instead of research into the cause, focusing more on the lives rather than any sort of “prevention.” ASAN believes that autistic individuals deserve equal rights and opportunities in every day life and in the modern day workplace. They aim to have the voice of every autistic to be heard.

About Autism

Autism is a development disability that affects how we see the world around us. Autism is not a type of condition or disease and is a normal part of who we are. People who are autistic are born that way and are autistic their whole lives. Autism falls heavily onto a spectrum of many different symptoms and quirks. There is no one way to be autistic, as many autistic people will act completely different from each other. Despite this, there are many common symptoms that all autistic individuals posses at some capacity.

Autism, and Masking at Work

Masking

Masking is when an autistic individual hides or suppress their symptoms in order to appear and fit in with their non-autistic peers.

Why do autistic people mask?

Many autistic people feel the need to mask to avoid being persecuted for their symptoms and to be more easily accepted by others

What does it look like?

Masking can appear as suppressing stimming or using more “acceptable” stims to use in public. It could also be forcing eye contact or physical contact, despite the discomfort it might cause. Many individuals will practice script they go through for different types of social interactions.

Masking can feel like a personal shield when individuals feel uncomfortable with how others perceive and look them.

What problems arise from consistent masking?

Constantly masking around others can lead to a loss of self the for the autistic individual. Physical and mental problems can quickly arise from non-stop masking.

Despite the relief it can bring people from not standing out too far from the crowd, Depression, anxiety, and internalized ableism can all sprout from consistent masking. Many will wonder if they are only accepted for how they act instead of who they actually are.

Our Campaign

Our campaign strives to create a better form of communication with those on the autism spectrum and those who are not. Most of the information we receive of autistic individuals only cover how they might act as children, not adults. Because of this, many assume it either goes away, or is grown out of. Rather than over explaining Autism and its many variables, which could isolate and ostracize members of the community, our campaign gives simple tips to help communicate and understand. The shirts and posters serve to help put the individual in the mindset of an autistic individual, and how they interrupt different actions and ideas.
